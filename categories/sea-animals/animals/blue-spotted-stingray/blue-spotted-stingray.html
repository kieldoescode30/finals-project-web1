General Information

This section offers a comprehensive overview of the Blue Spotted Stingray

Scientific Name:

Taeniura lymma

Type:

Species of stingray in the family Dasyatidae

Trophic Categorization:

The blue-spotted stingray (Taeniura lymma) is primarily categorized as a secondary or tertiary consumer in the trophic hierarchy of marine ecosystems. As an elasmobranch, it preys on a variety of bottom-dwelling invertebrates such as crustaceans, mollusks, and small fish, using its flattened body and wing-like fins to glide over the ocean floor in search of prey. While it primarily hunts for live prey, it may also scavenge on carrion when available. Being a predator in the marine food web, the blue-spotted stingray plays a crucial role in controlling the populations of its prey species, contributing to the overall balance and health of its ecosystem.

Place of Origin:

The blue-spotted stingray (Taeniura lymma) is commonly found in tropical and subtropical waters throughout the Indo-Pacific region. Its natural range extends from the eastern coast of Africa, including the Red Sea, to the western Pacific Ocean, including regions such as the Indian Ocean, the Arabian Sea, and the waters around Southeast Asia, including the Philippines, Indonesia, and northern Australia.

Sound of Animal:

Unknown

Description & Characteristics

The blue-spotted stingray (Taeniura lymma) is a visually striking species with a flattened body adorned with vibrant blue spots, inhabiting shallow coastal waters of the Indo-Pacific region. Typically reaching sizes of 50 to 70 centimeters in disc width, these rays are nocturnally active hunters, preying on small invertebrates and fish using their electroreceptive sensory organs. They are commonly found resting on the seafloor during the day, utilizing camouflage and quick swimming abilities as defense mechanisms against predators.
